The rules, in plain language

Vita Nova follows standardized natural-charting conventions — a method of Natural Family Planning fully in line with Catholic teaching. This page explains the code, the stickers, and how the fertile window guides decisions. It's a learning aid — to use the method for family planning, work with a trained instructor.

What is the VDRS code?

VDRS = the Vaginal Discharge Recording System. It's a precise, standardized shorthand so your chart means exactly the same thing to you and to a trained practitioner. Each day you record the single most fertile sign you saw, and it becomes a short code with up to three parts:

  1. Category number — how fertile the observation is (0 = nothing seen, up to 10 = clear & stretchy mucus).
  2. Descriptor letters — the colour & quality of any mucus (e.g. K clear, C cloudy, L lubricative).
  3. Frequency — how many times you saw it (×1, ×2, ×3, or AD = all day).

For example, 8KLx2 = tacky/gummy mucus that was clear and lubricative, seen twice. A bleeding day uses a single letter instead: H, M, L, VL, or B.

The stickers

Red — a day of menstrual bleeding.
Green — a dry day. A time of infertility.
👶White baby — mucus is present. A potentially fertile day; conception is possible.
👶Green baby — a dry post-Peak count day (1·2·3): dry-looking, but still counted as fertile.
Yellow — used in special situations (breastfeeding, pre-menopause) under a practitioner's guidance.
PPeak & count — Peak Day, then 1·2·3. These four days are always fertile.

Finding your Peak Day

The Peak Day is the last day you have mucus that is clear, stretchy (1″+), or lubricative. The catch: you can't know it was your Peak until the next day, when that slippery, fertile-type mucus is gone. So mark it retroactively — in Vita Nova every day stays editable, so just tap back to the day and tick "Peak Day" once you're sure. The app then counts the three days after it (1, 2, 3); the Peak plus those three days are always fertile.

Using the chart for family planning

A quick word on a term you'll see often: EOD means "End of Day" — i.e. in the evening, once the day's observations are complete.

To avoid pregnancy

  • Menstruation: bleeding can hide mucus, so days of flow are treated as fertile and avoided.
  • Pre-Peak dry days: on green (dry) days before ovulation, intercourse is taken in the evening (EOD) and only on an every-other-day basis. The skipped day lets you tell true mucus apart from leftover seminal fluid, which can mimic it.
  • Any mucus day, and Peak through Peak +3: fertile — avoided.
  • Post-Peak: from the evening of the 4th day after Peak onward, the rest of the cycle is infertile.

To achieve pregnancy

  • Focus on the mucus days — especially the most fertile, lubricative, stretchy days leading up to and including the Peak Day.
  • The Peak Day and the day on either side of it are typically the most fertile of the whole cycle.
  • Persistent unusual patterns (very short or long mucus phases, limited mucus) are worth reviewing with a trained instructor or a physician familiar with cycle charting.
